Air handlers are indoor HVAC units that circulate conditioned air throughout a building by housing the blower motor, air filter, and heat exchanger coil in a single cabinet. Air handlers operate as part of a split system, receiving cooled or heated refrigerant from an outdoor unit and distributing the treated air through ductwork. What an air handler is and how it works

An air handler (known as an AHU or air handling unit) is an indoor HVAC component that moves air across a coil to deliver heating or cooling to conditioned spaces. Air handlers are a subtype of HVAC equipment, distinct from ductless units and window units that contain their own refrigerant circuits. The blower motor inside the cabinet draws return air through the filter housing, passes it across the heat exchanger coil, and pushes the treated air into the supply duct network. An air conditioning contractor sizes the air handler to match the outdoor condensing unit so the system operates at rated efficiency. Air handlers pair directly with heat pumps, receiving both heating and cooling refrigerant cycles from the same outdoor unit depending on the season.

Measurable attributes of air handlers

Air handlers carry three primary measurable attributes that determine system performance. Airflow capacity, filter compatibility, and motor type are the attributes that define whether an air handler matches a given application.

  • Airflow capacity: Airflow capacity is rated in CFM (cubic feet per minute). A correctly sized air handler delivers enough CFM to match the tonnage of the outdoor unit, typically 400 CFM per ton of cooling capacity.
  • Filter size compatibility: Filter size compatibility is expressed in MERV ratings. Air handlers accept filters rated from MERV 8 to MERV 13 in standard residential configurations, with higher MERV ratings capturing finer particulates at the cost of increased static pressure.
  • Motor type: Motor type is either ECM (electronically commutated motor) or PSC (permanent split capacitor). ECM motors adjust blower speed in variable increments, reducing energy consumption by 30 to 60 percent compared to single-speed PSC motors.

Variable speed air handlers use ECM technology to modulate airflow in response to real-time load conditions. Systems using variable refrigerant flow technology pair with variable speed air handlers to achieve precise zone-by-zone temperature control across multi-room or multi-floor layouts.

How air handlers relate to the broader HVAC system

Air handlers are the indoor half of a split HVAC system, working in tandem with an outdoor condensing unit or heat pump. The air handler unit receives refrigerant through a set of refrigerant lines, one carrying high-pressure liquid and one carrying low-pressure vapor. The heat exchanger coil inside the air handler absorbs heat from return air during cooling mode and releases heat into supply air during heating mode. Without a properly matched air handler, the outdoor unit operates under excessive head pressure, shortening compressor life. Air handlers connect to the duct system at two points: the return plenum, which pulls unconditioned air in, and the supply plenum, which pushes treated air out to each room register.

Common air handler problems and signs of failure

Air handler problems present through four primary failure modes that affect comfort, air quality, and energy consumption. Blower motor failure, coil icing, filter restriction, and drain pan overflow are the 4 most common air handler faults in Clearwater's high-humidity climate.

  • Blower motor failure: Blower motor failure produces weak or absent airflow at registers. A failed PSC motor draws locked-rotor amperage, tripping the circuit breaker within 2 to 5 minutes of startup.
  • Coil icing: Coil icing forms when airflow drops below the minimum CFM threshold or refrigerant charge falls below design pressure. Ice buildup on the heat exchanger coil blocks airflow entirely within 4 to 8 hours of onset.
  • Filter restriction: Filter restriction occurs when a MERV 11 or higher filter loads with particulates and static pressure across the filter exceeds 0.5 inches of water column. Restricted filters force the blower motor to draw 15 to 25 percent more amperage to maintain target CFM.
  • Drain pan overflow: Drain pan overflow results from algae growth blocking the condensate drain line. A blocked drain line causes the secondary float switch to cut power to the air handler within 30 to 90 minutes of water accumulation, shutting the system down to prevent ceiling or floor damage.

Ignoring air handler faults accelerates wear on the outdoor compressor, raises monthly energy costs, and degrades indoor air quality through mold growth on a wet coil surface. Poulin Cooling A/C and Refrigeration diagnoses air handler faults using static pressure testing, amperage measurement, and refrigerant pressure checks to isolate the root cause before recommending repair or replacement.

Where air handlers apply in Clearwater properties

Air handlers serve residential, light commercial, and refrigeration applications wherever a ducted split system delivers conditioned air. Residential air handlers are installed in attics, closets, or utility rooms and range from 1.5-ton to 5-ton capacity for single-family homes. Light commercial air handlers serve retail spaces, offices, and restaurants, with units sized from 5 tons to 25 tons depending on square footage and internal heat load. Refrigeration-grade air handlers circulate air inside walk-in coolers and freezers, operating at coil temperatures between 25°F and 35°F for coolers and between -10°F and 10°F for freezers. In Clearwater's subtropical climate, air handlers run an average of 2,800 to 3,200 hours per year, making annual coil cleaning, drain line flushing, and blower wheel inspection essential maintenance tasks for preserving rated CFM output and system efficiency.
