What a coolant leak is in an AC system

A coolant leak is a condition in which refrigerant fluid escapes through an unintended opening in the sealed AC system. Coolant leaks belong to the broader category of HVAC system malfunctions that a licensed air conditioning contractor diagnoses and repairs. The three component parts of every coolant leak are the refrigerant fluid itself, the leak source point, and the escape pathway through which refrigerant exits the system. A sealed system with a proper refrigerant charge is the direct contrast to a coolant leak condition. Coolant leaks go by several alternate names, including refrigerant leak, Freon leak, and AC leak.

Measurable attributes of a coolant leak

Coolant leak severity is measured across three primary attributes: flow rate, location, and severity classification. Flow rate ranges from drops per minute in minor seepage to a continuous stream in a major rupture. Location defines where refrigerant escapes, with the 3 most common sites being connection points, evaporator coils, and refrigerant lines. Severity classification spans from minor seepage at one end to major rupture at the other. A frozen evaporator coil is a direct downstream consequence of low refrigerant caused by a leak at the coil surface. Poulin Cooling A/C and Refrigeration measures each of these attributes during a diagnostic inspection to determine the correct repair scope.

How a coolant leak relates to the broader HVAC system

A coolant leak is a type of HVAC system malfunction that disrupts the refrigeration cycle at the point of escape. Coolant leaks cause reduced cooling performance as the first measurable system effect. Reduced refrigerant charge forces the compressor to work harder, shortening its operational lifespan. A coolant leak that goes unrepaired progresses from a performance issue to a component failure issue. An AC not cooling is the most common symptom reported by homeowners before a coolant leak is formally diagnosed. In severe cases, a coolant leak requires full system evaluation to determine whether AC unit replacement is more cost-effective than continued repair.

Signs and problems caused by a coolant leak

Coolant leak signs appear across 4 measurable categories: temperature output, equipment behavior, physical evidence, and secondary damage. The 4 signs and the problems each one indicates are listed below.

  • Warm air from vents: Warm air output signals that refrigerant charge has dropped below the level needed to absorb heat from indoor air.
  • Ice buildup on coils: Ice formation on the evaporator coil indicates refrigerant pressure has fallen low enough to cause the coil surface to drop below freezing, which stresses condensate drain lines as ice melts and overwhelms the condensate system.
  • Hissing or bubbling sounds: Audible hissing near the refrigerant lines or connection points indicates active refrigerant escape through a crack or loose fitting.
  • Higher energy bills: A system running with low refrigerant charge operates longer per cycle to reach the set temperature, increasing electricity consumption measurably.

Where coolant leaks occur in AC equipment

Coolant leaks occur at 3 primary locations within an air conditioning system. The 3 locations and the failure mechanism at each are described below.

  • Connection points: Connection points between refrigerant lines and system components loosen over time from vibration and thermal expansion, creating gaps through which refrigerant escapes.
  • Evaporator coils: Evaporator coils develop pinhole leaks from formicary corrosion, a chemical reaction between copper, moisture, and volatile organic compounds present in indoor air.
  • Refrigerant lines: Refrigerant lines sustain physical damage from rubbing against structural surfaces, pest activity, or improper installation, producing escape pathways along the line length.
