### What duct sealing is and how it works

Duct sealing is an HVAC maintenance process that closes air leaks in the ductwork system using mastic sealant, metallic foil tape, or aerosol-based sealant injected under pressure. The process follows 3 main phases: surface preparation, sealant application, and curing. Surface preparation removes dust, grease, and loose debris so the sealant bonds directly to the duct material. Sealant application covers every identified gap, seam, and joint with a continuous, airtight layer. The curing phase completes the seal, with full cure times ranging from 1 to 24 hours depending on sealant type and ambient conditions.

Duct sealing is a type of [ductwork](ductwork.md) maintenance that directly reduces CFM25 leakage, the industry-standard measurement of air escaping through duct walls at 25 pascals of pressure. A sealed duct system delivers conditioned air to the intended registers instead of losing it to unconditioned attic or wall cavities. The application temperature range for most approved sealants is 32°F to 120°F, making Clearwater's climate compatible with year-round sealing work.

### Measurable attributes of duct sealing

Duct sealing performance is quantified by CFM25 leakage reduction, the single most reliable metric for evaluating how much air a duct system loses before delivery. A pre-seal duct leakage test establishes the baseline CFM25 reading. A post-seal test confirms the reduction achieved after sealant application and curing. The curing window of 1 to 24 hours must complete before post-seal testing produces accurate results.

The application temperature range of 32°F to 120°F defines the conditions under which sealant bonds correctly to duct surfaces. Applying sealant outside this range causes adhesion failure, premature cracking, and return leakage within months. Mastic sealant applied within the correct temperature window maintains flexibility through thermal expansion cycles, which is critical in Clearwater where attic temperatures regularly exceed 130°F in summer. An [air conditioning contractor](air-conditioning-contractor.md) with verified duct sealing experience selects the correct sealant formulation for the installation environment and confirms CFM25 readings before and after the work.

### How duct sealing relates to the broader HVAC system

Duct sealing is a component of the larger HVAC maintenance process that includes equipment servicing, filtration, and indoor air quality management. Sealed ductwork reduces the static pressure load on the air handler, which lowers blower motor strain and extends equipment service life. Leaking ducts draw unconditioned, humid air from attics and crawlspaces into the supply stream, raising indoor humidity levels and increasing the demand on any installed [dehumidifier](dehumidifier.md).

Duct sealing affects filtration performance. A leaking return duct bypasses the filter entirely, pulling unfiltered air directly into the air handler. This unfiltered air carries particulates that accumulate on coils, blower wheels, and [HEPA filter](hepa-filter.md) media, reducing their rated efficiency and shortening replacement intervals. Sealing the return duct forces all return air through the designated filter path, restoring the filtration system to its rated performance. [Air duct cleaning](air-duct-cleaning.md) performed after duct sealing removes accumulated debris from sealed duct interiors, completing the restoration of airflow quality.

### Common signs that duct sealing is needed

Duct sealing is needed when the HVAC system shows 4 primary indicators: uneven room temperatures, elevated energy bills without a change in usage, excessive dust accumulation near registers, and audible hissing from duct runs during system operation. Uneven room temperatures indicate that supply air is escaping before reaching distant registers, leaving those zones under-conditioned. Elevated energy bills reflect the longer run times required when the system compensates for lost conditioned air.

Excessive dust near registers signals that unfiltered air is entering the duct system through unsealed return leaks and depositing particulates at supply outlets. Audible hissing from duct runs identifies the specific leak locations where pressurized air forces through gaps at seams and joints. The consequence of ignoring these signs is progressive leakage growth, because unsealed gaps expand under repeated thermal cycling, increasing CFM25 leakage rates and compounding efficiency losses each cooling season.

- **Uneven room temperatures:** uneven room temperatures confirm that supply air is escaping duct seams before reaching the intended register

- **Elevated energy bills:** elevated energy bills reflect extended run times caused by conditioned air lost to unconditioned spaces

- **Excessive dust at registers:** excessive dust at registers indicates unfiltered air entering through unsealed return duct leaks

- **Audible hissing from duct runs:** audible hissing from duct runs pinpoints active leak sites at joints and seams under system pressure

- **High indoor humidity:** high indoor humidity results from humid attic air drawn into the supply stream through unsealed return leaks

### Where duct sealing applies in residential and commercial systems

Duct sealing applies to supply ducts, return ducts, air handler connections, and plenum joints across residential and commercial HVAC systems. Supply duct leaks lose conditioned air to unconditioned spaces, reducing delivery to occupied zones. Return duct leaks draw unconditioned air into the system, raising the load on both the cooling equipment and any installed dehumidification. Air handler connections are the highest-pressure points in the system and the most common location for sealant failure in aging installations.

Plenum joints, where the main trunk connects to the air handler cabinet, experience the greatest pressure differential and require mastic sealant rather than tape alone for a durable seal. In Clearwater's climate, attic-mounted duct systems face the most severe thermal cycling, with duct surface temperatures swinging from 65°F in winter to over 120°F in summer.
