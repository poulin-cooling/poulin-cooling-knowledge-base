### What a frozen evaporator coil is in an HVAC system

A frozen evaporator coil (known as an iced evaporator coil or frozen cooling coil) is a condition where ice accumulates on the coil fins, refrigerant lines, and surrounding components inside the air handler. The evaporator coil is the component responsible for absorbing heat from indoor air as refrigerant passes through it. A frozen evaporator coil belongs to the broader category of [HVAC system malfunctions](air-conditioning-contractor.md) that require professional diagnosis to resolve safely.

The coil operates by allowing warm indoor air to pass over refrigerant-filled tubes. When airflow drops or refrigerant pressure falls below the correct range, the coil surface temperature drops below 32°F. Moisture from the air then freezes directly onto the fins and refrigerant lines, forming ice buildup that compounds the original problem by further restricting airflow.

### Measurable attributes of a frozen evaporator coil

A frozen evaporator coil produces three measurable changes in system performance. Ice thickness on the coil surface reaches 1 to 5 mm before visible symptoms appear at the supply vents. Airflow through the air handler drops to between 0% and 50% of normal rated capacity as ice blocks the fin passages. The coil surface temperature falls below 32°F, which is the direct physical cause of ice formation on the fins and refrigerant lines.

These three values, ice thickness, airflow percentage, and surface temperature, are the diagnostic benchmarks a technician measures to confirm a frozen coil versus other causes of reduced cooling. [Reduced cooling performance](ac-not-cooling.md) is the primary symptom a homeowner notices before the coil is visually inspected. A temperature differential reading below 32°F at the coil confirms the freeze condition and separates it from a dirty filter or failed blower motor.

### How a frozen evaporator coil relates to the broader refrigeration system

A frozen evaporator coil is a direct consequence of abnormal [refrigerant](refrigerant.md) pressure or flow inside the system. Refrigerant circulates through the evaporator coil at a pressure calibrated to produce a coil surface temperature above 32°F under normal airflow conditions. A drop in refrigerant charge, caused by a leak or improper fill, lowers the boiling point of the refrigerant and drives the coil surface below the freezing threshold.

A frozen evaporator coil causes reduced cooling capacity throughout the conditioned space. The ice layer acts as insulation, preventing warm return air from transferring heat into the refrigerant. The system runs continuously without reaching the thermostat setpoint, increasing compressor runtime and energy consumption. A [coolant leak](coolant-leak.md) is one of the most common upstream causes that leads directly to a frozen coil condition.

### Common causes and signs of a frozen evaporator coil

A frozen evaporator coil develops from four primary causes. Restricted airflow, low refrigerant charge, a malfunctioning blower motor, and blocked drain lines each reduce the coil's ability to stay above 32°F during operation.

- **Restricted airflow:** Restricted airflow from a clogged filter or closed supply vents reduces the volume of warm air passing over the coil, allowing the surface temperature to fall below 32°F within one to four hours of operation.

- **Low refrigerant charge:** Low refrigerant charge lowers the suction pressure inside the coil, dropping the refrigerant boiling point and freezing the coil surface even when airflow is normal.

- **Malfunctioning blower motor:** A malfunctioning blower motor reduces airflow to 0% to 50% of rated capacity, producing the same freeze condition as a clogged filter without any visible obstruction at the filter.

- **Blocked drain lines:** [Blocked drain lines](clogged-drain-lines.md) allow condensate to back up around the coil, adding moisture that accelerates ice formation once the coil surface drops below 32°F.

The visible signs of a frozen evaporator coil include ice visible on the refrigerant lines near the air handler, warm air at the supply vents despite the system running, and water pooling around the indoor unit as the ice melts during the off cycle. Airflow at the supply registers drops noticeably when ice blocks 50% or more of the fin surface area.
