### What poor indoor air quality is

Poor indoor air quality is an indoor environmental problem defined by pollutant concentrations that exceed healthy limits inside an occupied space. The condition belongs to the broader category of indoor environmental problems and contrasts directly with a healthy indoor environment. Three component pollutants drive the condition: particulate matter, volatile organic compounds (VOCs), and carbon dioxide. Particulate matter includes fine particles at or below 2.5 microns in diameter. VOCs include off-gassing from paints, adhesives, and cleaning products. Carbon dioxide accumulates when ventilation rates fall below occupancy demand. Poor indoor air quality is referred to as substandard indoor air or unhealthy indoor air in technical literature.

### Measurable attributes of poor indoor air quality

Poor indoor air quality carries three primary measurable thresholds that define when a space crosses from acceptable to substandard. PM2.5 concentration above 12 µg/m³ marks the first threshold for particulate-driven poor air quality. Carbon dioxide concentration above 1,000 ppm indicates inadequate ventilation and elevated occupant-generated CO2. VOC concentration above 500 µg/m³ signals chemical off-gassing at levels associated with occupant discomfort. These three Entity-Attribute-Value benchmarks allow technicians to diagnose poor indoor air quality with instruments rather than symptoms alone. ### How poor indoor air quality relates to HVAC systems

Poor indoor air quality directly affects air conditioning systems by forcing them to circulate contaminated air through ductwork, coils, and filters. The HVAC system is both a pathway for pollutant distribution and the primary mechanical treatment for poor indoor air. A system with a clogged or degraded filter redistributes particulate matter rather than capturing it. A system with oversized cooling capacity removes heat faster than moisture, leaving relative humidity elevated and creating conditions for mold spore proliferation. ### Common signs and sources of poor indoor air quality

Poor indoor air quality produces recognizable physical signs inside a building before instrument readings are taken. The 4 most common observable indicators are persistent dust accumulation on supply registers, visible mold growth near return vents, stale or chemical odors that intensify after the air handler runs, and condensation on interior window surfaces. Each sign maps to a measurable pollutant class. Dust on registers indicates PM2.5 concentrations above the 12 µg/m³ threshold. Mold near vents indicates humidity-driven biological growth. Chemical odors indicate VOC concentrations approaching or exceeding 500 µg/m³. Condensation indicates relative humidity above 60% RH. A [dirty air filter](https://poulincoolingacandrefrigeration.mintlify.app/dirty-air-filter) is one of the most frequent mechanical sources of elevated particulate matter indoors, because a saturated filter bypasses particles back into the supply stream. An [AC not cooling](https://poulincoolingacandrefrigeration.mintlify.app/ac-not-cooling) properly often runs longer cycles that pull more unconditioned outdoor air through gaps in the building envelope, introducing additional particulates and humidity. The sources of poor indoor air quality include:

- **Clogged filtration:** Clogged filtration allows PM2.5 particles to bypass the filter media and recirculate through supply ducts.

- **Inadequate ventilation:** Inadequate ventilation allows CO2 to accumulate above 1,000 ppm in occupied rooms with low air exchange rates.

- **Excess humidity:** Excess humidity above 60% RH accelerates mold growth and VOC off-gassing from flooring, insulation, and adhesives.

- **Duct leakage:** Duct leakage draws unconditioned attic or crawlspace air, along with its particulate load, into the conditioned supply stream.

- **Oversized or undersized equipment:** Oversized or undersized equipment creates short-cycling or extended run times that prevent stable temperature and humidity control.
