### What tonnage is in air conditioning systems

Tonnage is a numerical designation that expresses how much heat an air conditioning system removes per hour. The measurement originates from the amount of heat required to melt one ton of ice over 24 hours, which equals 12,000 BTU per hour. [BTU, the base unit of heat energy](doc:btu), is the foundation from which every tonnage rating is derived. A 2-ton system removes 24,000 BTU per hour. A 3-ton system removes 36,000 BTU per hour. A 5-ton system removes 60,000 BTU per hour.

Tonnage is a product attribute, not a performance rating. The numerical scale runs from 0.5 tons for small ductless units to 50 tons or more for large commercial equipment. Tonnage determines which equipment class a system belongs to, which in turn determines the refrigerant line size, duct diameter, and electrical circuit requirements for the installation.

### Measurable attributes of tonnage and how they are expressed

Tonnage carries three primary measurable attributes that define its role in system specification. The cooling capacity range spans 0.5 tons to 50 or more tons across the full product spectrum. The heat removal rate is fixed at 12,000 BTU per hour for every single ton of capacity. The system size designation is expressed as a whole or half-number on a numerical scale, such as 1.5, 2, 2.5, 3, 3.5, 4, or 5 tons for residential equipment.

Tonnage is encoded directly into the model number of most air conditioning equipment. A model number containing "024" indicates a 24,000 BTU system, which equals 2 tons. A model number containing "036" indicates a 36,000 BTU system, which equals 3 tons. [A load calculation confirms the required tonnage](doc:load-calculation) before any equipment is selected, ensuring the model number matches the actual demand of the structure.

### How tonnage relates to the broader HVAC system

Tonnage determines the physical and electrical specifications of every component in an air conditioning system. The refrigerant line set diameter, the air handler size, the duct cross-section, and the breaker amperage all scale directly with the tonnage rating. A 3-ton system requires a larger air handler, wider duct trunks, and a higher-amperage circuit than a 2-ton system serving the same structure.

Tonnage contrasts with SEER rating, which measures energy efficiency rather than cooling output. A system with a high tonnage removes more heat per hour. [A system with a high SEER rating removes the same heat while consuming less electricity.](doc:seer-rating) Both attributes appear on every equipment specification sheet, and both must be evaluated together during system selection. Tonnage answers "how much cooling," and SEER answers "at what energy cost."

Tonnage determines the refrigerant charge volume. A 2-ton system holds a smaller refrigerant charge than a 4-ton system. Overcharging or undercharging refrigerant relative to the rated tonnage reduces system efficiency and accelerates compressor wear. [A licensed air conditioning contractor](doc:air-conditioning-contractor) verifies the refrigerant charge against the manufacturer's tonnage specification during every service visit.

### Common problems caused by incorrect tonnage

Tonnage mismatches produce two distinct failure patterns in air conditioning systems. **Oversizing** and **undersizing** are the two categories of tonnage error, and each creates a separate set of symptoms and long-term damage.

- **Short cycling:** Short cycling occurs when an oversized system cools the space too quickly, shuts off before completing a full run cycle, and restarts within minutes. A system that short cycles runs 8 to 12 cycles per hour instead of the standard 2 to 3 cycles, which accelerates compressor wear and increases energy consumption.

- **High indoor humidity:** High indoor humidity results from short cycling because the evaporator coil does not run long enough to condense and drain moisture from the air. Relative humidity above 60 percent indoors signals a potential oversizing problem in Florida's climate.

- **Inability to reach setpoint:** Inability to reach the thermostat setpoint on peak summer days indicates an undersized system. A 2-ton system installed in a space requiring 3 tons runs continuously without satisfying the thermostat when outdoor temperatures exceed 90 degrees Fahrenheit.

- **Elevated energy bills:** Elevated energy bills accompany both oversizing and undersizing. An oversized system draws high startup amperage repeatedly throughout the day. An undersized system runs without stopping, accumulating runtime hours far beyond the design expectation.

- **Premature compressor failure:** Premature compressor failure follows extended periods of either short cycling or continuous operation. Compressors in correctly sized systems last 12 to 15 years. Compressors in mismatched systems fail in 5 to 8 years under the same maintenance conditions.

### Where tonnage applies across cooling system categories

Tonnage applies across every category of mechanical cooling equipment, from residential split systems to commercial rooftop units and industrial process chillers. Residential split systems in Clearwater most commonly fall in the 2-ton to 5-ton range, with 3-ton and 3.5-ton systems covering the majority of single-family homes between 1,400 and 2,200 square feet under Florida's high-humidity load conditions.

Ductless mini-split systems express tonnage in the same BTU-per-hour scale. A 12,000 BTU mini-split equals 1 ton. A 24,000 BTU mini-split equals 2 tons. Multi-zone mini-split systems aggregate the tonnage of each indoor air handler to determine the total capacity of the outdoor condenser unit.

Commercial rooftop units (packaged units) range from 3 tons to 25 tons for mid-size commercial buildings. Chiller systems serving large commercial and industrial facilities measure capacity in tons of refrigeration, with units ranging from 20 tons to several hundred tons.
