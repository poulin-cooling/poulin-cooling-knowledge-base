### What a blower motor is in an HVAC system

A blower motor is an electric motor that drives a fan to move air across the evaporator coil and through the duct network of an HVAC system. Blower motors belong to the broader category of electric motors and are called HVAC blowers, furnace blower motors, or circulator motors. The motor connects to a shaft, which turns fan blades housed inside a motor housing. [A licensed air conditioning contractor](doc:air-conditioning-contractor) selects the correct motor class based on the system's voltage and horsepower requirements. Without a functioning blower motor, conditioned air stays trapped at the air handler and never reaches the occupied space.

### Measurable attributes of blower motors

Blower motors carry three primary measurable attributes that determine compatibility with an HVAC system. Power rating, rotational speed, and supply voltage are the 3 attributes that define a blower motor's operating specification.

- **Power rating:** Power rating spans 1/4 HP to 5 HP. Residential systems typically use motors in the 1/3 HP to 1 HP range, while commercial air handlers require motors rated at 2 HP to 5 HP.

- **Rotational speed:** Rotational speed ranges from 850 RPM to 3,450 RPM. Multi-speed and variable-speed motors adjust RPM to match the airflow demand of the space, reducing energy draw at partial load.

- **Supply voltage:** Supply voltage is 115V for smaller residential motors, 230V for standard split systems, and 460V for large commercial units. Mismatched voltage destroys a motor within minutes of startup.

Blower motors in [heat pump systems](doc:heat-pumps) must handle both heating and cooling cycles, which places higher cumulative run-hour demands on the motor than a cooling-only system.

### How blower motors relate to the broader HVAC system

Blower motors power air circulation and drive the fan that moves air across the evaporator coil, making them a direct functional component of the air handler assembly. The blower motor sits inside the air handler, which connects upstream to the return duct and downstream to the supply duct network. [Condenser units](doc:condenser-units) reject heat to the outdoors, but the blower motor is what delivers the cooled air from the indoor coil to the occupied space. Without the blower motor, the refrigeration cycle in the condenser unit runs but produces no comfort benefit inside the building.

Blower motors in [ductless mini split systems](doc:ductless-mini-split-systems) are housed entirely within the indoor wall unit, eliminating the duct network but retaining the same motor-to-fan relationship. Systems using [variable refrigerant flow](doc:variable-refrigerant-flow) technology pair variable-speed blower motors with modulating compressors to match airflow and refrigerant delivery simultaneously, improving efficiency across partial-load conditions.

### Common blower motor problems and signs of failure

Blower motor failure produces 4 recognizable signs that indicate the motor requires inspection or replacement. Weak airflow, no airflow, unusual noise, and overheating are the primary failure indicators.

- **Weak airflow:** Weak airflow signals a motor running below its rated RPM, often caused by a failing capacitor, worn bearings, or a dirty squirrel-cage fan restricting rotation.

- **No airflow:** No airflow with the system otherwise running points to a seized motor, a blown run capacitor, or an open winding inside the motor coil.

- **Unusual noise:** Unusual noise from the air handler, including squealing, grinding, or rattling, indicates bearing wear, a loose shaft, or debris contacting the fan blades at speed.

- **Overheating:** Overheating causes the motor's thermal overload protector to trip, shutting the motor off mid-cycle. A motor that trips repeatedly requires replacement, not reset.

Ignoring blower motor failure accelerates damage to the evaporator coil. A coil that freezes over from zero airflow takes 24 to 48 hours to thaw fully before the system returns to service. ### Where blower motors apply across system types

Blower motors apply across every forced-air HVAC system type, including central split systems, packaged rooftop units, air handlers, and furnaces. Central split systems use a single blower motor rated at 1/3 HP to 1 HP to serve the entire duct system of a residential structure. Packaged rooftop units used in commercial applications house blower motors rated at 1 HP to 5 HP inside a single weatherproof cabinet. Furnaces use a blower motor to distribute heated air through the same duct network that the cooling system uses in summer. Variable-speed electronically commutated motors (ECMs) replace single-speed motors in high-efficiency systems, adjusting speed in increments as small as 1 RPM to maintain precise airflow at each register.
